# Day 2: Decode Your Thoughts – The Thought Trap

## Lesson Content

Today, we're talking about a sneaky little thing called Negative Automatic Thoughts, or NATs for short. These are those quick, unhelpful thoughts that pop into your mind during social situations. They're like little brain ninjas—fast, sneaky, and often wrong.

Let's say you're walking into a party. Before you even say hi, your brain might blurt out, 'Everyone's staring at me,' or 'I'm going to embarrass myself.' Sound familiar? Those are NATs in action. The tricky thing is, NATs feel so real that we don't even question them, but they're often exaggerations or just plain false. And the more we believe them, the more anxious we feel.

So, why do we have NATs? They're actually your brain's way of trying to protect you. Back in caveman days, we needed to notice danger—like a tiger in the bushes. Now, your brain still scans for threats, but instead of tigers, it's overthinking social situations. The problem? NATs make harmless situations feel like life-or-death battles.

But here's the good news: NATs are just thoughts, not facts. Once we learn to spot them, we can start taking their power away. That's what today is all about—decoding your thoughts so you can figure out which ones are helping you and which ones are holding you back.

## Journal Prompts

- What's a recent NAT you've noticed?
- What's the evidence for this thought?
- What's the evidence against this thought?
- What would you say to a friend who had this thought?

## Daily Exercise

Start spotting your NATs. Don't worry about fixing them yet—just notice when they pop up. We'll build on this tomorrow.

Use the "Spot Your NATs Worksheet" to document:
- Situation: Describe the social situation
- NAT: Write the negative automatic thought
- Feeling: How did the thought make you feel? (Use emoji rankings for intensity)

## Thought Traps Reference Guide

Common thought traps that fuel NATs:
- Mind Reading: "They think I'm boring."
- Catastrophizing: "If I mess up, it'll ruin everything."
- All-or-Nothing Thinking: "If I don't do it perfectly, I'll fail."

Circle any traps you've noticed in your own thoughts.

## Motivation

You're not your thoughts—you're the one in charge of them. Remember, noticing your NATs is a huge first step!

Your thoughts aren't facts. Learning to spot them is like finding the keys to your confidence.
