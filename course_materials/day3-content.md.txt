# Day 3: Flip the Script – Changing Negative Automatic Thoughts (NATs)

## Lesson Content

Today, we're going to take those negative automatic thoughts—those NATs we talked about—and flip them! Think of it like taking a bad meme and making it funny—it's all about perspective.

Changing how you think isn't easy. NATs can feel like they're set in stone because they've been hanging out in your brain for so long. But here's the truth: NATs are habits, not facts. Like any habit, they can be unlearned. Let's dive into how.

Step 1: Spot the thought. We practiced this yesterday. Now, we question it. Ask: 'Is this thought helpful? Is it true?' Then, we reframe it into something more balanced. It's not about being overly positive—it's about being realistic.

Let's pause here with an example. Zoe's NAT is, 'Everyone will think I'm cringy.' Now, let's question it. How does Zoe know what everyone will think? She doesn't! Most people are probably scrolling, liking, and moving on. Now, let's reframe: 'Some people might like it, and that's what matters.'

Why are NATs so sticky? Because they're connected to our belief systems—the deeper ideas we hold about ourselves and the world. Maybe you grew up thinking, 'I need to be perfect to be liked,' or 'If I make a mistake, people won't respect me.' These beliefs influence our NATs, but they're not permanent. The first step to changing them is becoming aware of them.

When we challenge our NATs, we're also challenging those old beliefs. It's like planting new seeds: 'I'm worthy, even if I make mistakes.' Over time, these new beliefs help us feel less anxious and more confident.

## Journal Prompts

- What's a recent NAT you experienced? How did you reframe it (or how could you)?
- What beliefs might be underneath your common NATs?
- What new belief would you like to adopt instead?

## Daily Exercise

Complete the "Thought-Flipping Practice Sheet":
1. Situation: Describe a recent social scenario
2. NAT: Write the negative thought you had
3. Question: What's the evidence for and against this thought?
4. Reframe: Write a more balanced or helpful thought

Example:
- Situation: "Presenting in class."
- NAT: "Everyone will think I'm dumb."
- Question: "Most people aren't paying that much attention. I've prepared well."
- Reframe: "I might make a mistake, but I've got this overall."

## Belief System Awareness

Take time to explore your underlying beliefs:
- What are three beliefs you hold about yourself in social situations?
- Where do you think these beliefs come from (e.g., family, past experiences)?
- Which belief would you like to challenge or change?

## Motivation

When we challenge our NATs, we're also challenging those old beliefs. It's like planting new seeds: 'I'm worthy, even if I make mistakes.' Over time, these new beliefs help us feel less anxious and more confident.

It won't feel natural at first, but like learning a TikTok dance, practice makes progress. You've got this!
